Proposition: NYPD racial bias is likely.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Use raw counts. Score: -0.5 I considered using per capita but I felt raw counts would be simpler and easier to grasp for the average person. I also considered using percentage of counts (e.g. ~60% for Black Americans), but the raw numbers felt more compelling.
  • Descending order. Score: -0.5. Sorting pushes highest value to the left, so the disparity is instantly recognizable. It also frames the story by leading with the extreme.
  • Red gradient. Score: -1 Red can connote violence, harm, and anger. Darker red can indicate greater severity. A neutral color like grey or blue would be more earnest but less impactful.
  • Y-axis starts at zero. Score: 1. A true zero baseline adds credibility and support trustworthy reading.
  • Loaded headline and subtitles. Score: -2. Strong initial statements can steer interpretation before the reader looks at the rest of the chart. The words “more than all others combined” further indicates a significant difference.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Stacked bar chart normalized to 100%. Score: -2. This normalization only shows the proportions of the complaint compositions, and hides the differences in amounts, making the groups appear similar.
  • Omitted sample sizes. Score -2. Not including sample size prevents keeps attention on composition and reduces transparency.
  • Pastel-like colors This downplays severity and may subtly communicate that nothing is wrong.
  • Loaded headline and subtitles. Score: -2. Similar to above, strong initial statements can steer interpretation before the reader looks at the rest of the chart. The words “relatively uncommon” in title description further downplays the severity.

Final Reflection

Seeking to tell opposing stories with the same dataset was rather challenging. I began by changing surface level elements (chart vs bubble chart, linear vs log scale) and quickly realized that these were not strong enough to change the narrative.

The real change came from reframing the question and shifting the focus entirely away from certain aspects of the data. In my case, focusing on the number of reports can tell one story, while focusing on the composition can tell another.

This exercise made me pay attention to what is NOT shown. A single chart can typically only show one X and one Y, with extra elements like color, labels and loaded titles. However, a dataset can contain dozens of fields. Decisions in framing the question will emphasize some facts and hide others.
